The Job Market Isn't Broken By Technology. It's Broken By People. And The System They Built Around Themselves.

The job market isn't broken because of AI. It's broken because of people. And design. Every day, hiring announcements and desperate job-seeker posts sit side by side.

The job market isn't broken because of AI. It's broken because of people. And design.

Every day I scroll through LinkedIn and I see two things sitting right next to each other. A company announcing they are hiring. And a person — qualified, experienced, desperate — announcing they are not.

People writing posts they never imagined they would write. Sharing their CVs publicly. Counting rejections. Begging for referrals. Hearts on their sleeve, dignity quietly negotiating with survival.

And I ask myself — how did we get here?

Because this is not an AI problem. AI didn't create this. People did. And the system they built around themselves did.

I want to talk about that system today. Not to vent. Not to point fingers at one villain. But because understanding what is actually broken is the only way anything ever gets fixed.

There are four walls holding this broken system up.

And until we are honest about all four — nothing changes.

The First Wall. The Algorithm That Replaced Human Judgment.

Somewhere along the way, hiring became a filtering problem. Too many applications. Too little time. So we built systems to sort them. ATS platforms that screen resumes before a single human being lays eyes on them. Keyword matching that decides in seconds whether fifteen years of experience is worth a conversation. Automated rejections sent before the hiring manager has even opened the role brief.

I understand the logic. Scale demands systems.

But here is what we lost in that trade.

The person who built something extraordinary but described it differently than the algorithm expected. The career changer whose transferable skills don't map neatly onto a job description written by a committee. The experienced professional whose resume doesn't contain the exact phrase the system was trained to find — not because they lack the skill, but because they called it something else.

We didn't build a filter. We built a wall.

And then we walked away and called it efficiency.

The Second Wall. The Theatre Of Hiring.

Easy Apply was supposed to democratise access. One click and your profile reaches the hiring manager. Simple. Fast. Human.

What it actually created was the illusion of hiring at industrial scale.

One click became ten clicks. Ten became a hundred. The same resume disappearing into the same void, over and over, with no confirmation that anyone received it, read it, or cared.

And LinkedIn — the platform at the centre of all of this — has quietly, steadily shifted from a hiring network into a content and data platform. Hiring posts generate impressions. Open roles drive engagement. Sponsored listings feed the algorithm. The business of LinkedIn is no longer purely connecting talent with opportunity. It is keeping everyone on the platform, producing content, generating data, staying visible.

Which is fine. Platforms evolve.

But nobody told the job seeker that the rules had changed.

They are still playing a hiring game on a platform that has quietly become something else. Still applying with sincerity into a system that processes them as numbers. Still waiting for a response that the design of the platform was never optimised to deliver.

The Third Wall. The People Who Stopped Showing Up.

This one is the most uncomfortable to talk about. Because it is the most human failure of all four.

Hiring managers who disappear after three rounds of interviews. Recruiters who promise to follow up and never do. Leaders who post "we are looking for exceptional talent" and don't respond to a single message from someone exceptional who reached out. HR teams sending rejections six months later — or never sending them at all.

I am not talking about cruelty. Most of these people are not cruel.

I am talking about a culture that slowly, quietly normalised the idea that the person on the other side of the application stopped being a person. They became a profile. A submission. A stage in a pipeline. Something to be processed, advanced, or rejected — without the basic dignity of an honest conversation.

Do you know what it actually costs someone to apply for a job?

Not money. Something far more expensive.

Time. Hope. The quiet reorganisation of their self-worth around whether a stranger decides they are enough. The anxiety of waiting. The recalibration required every single time silence arrives instead of an answer. The daily effort of keeping their confidence intact through a process specifically designed to make them feel replaceable.

We stopped accounting for that cost a long time ago.

And the people running the system never felt it. So they never fixed it.

The Fourth Wall. Built For Volume. Not For Humans.

The entire architecture of modern hiring was designed with one objective — process more, faster.

More applications. More screening rounds. More assessments. More data points. More stages between the human being and the decision.

Nobody stopped to ask the harder question.

Are we actually finding better people? Are we treating them better? Are we building something that works for the person on both sides of this table — not just the company's need to feel thorough?

The answer, if we are being honest, is no.

We built a machine that is extraordinarily efficient at processing people. And extraordinarily bad at seeing them.

So What Now?

I am not writing this to be cynical. I have spent twenty years inside this system — as a marketer, a leader, a builder — and I still believe in the possibility of getting this right.

But getting it right requires honesty. From both sides.

To every company, hiring manager, recruiter, and leader reading this:

The person on the other side of that application is not a data point. They researched your company. They rewrote their resume. They pressed submit with a quiet hope that this time might be different.

One response costs you nothing. One honest rejection — even a template — restores something that silence took away. One DM answered can change the direction of someone's week, month, career.

You have more power in this system than you realise.

Use it like a human being.

To every person navigating this right now:

The market is broken. The system is broken. The design is broken.

You are not.

Your experience is real. Your skills are real. Your value does not expire because an algorithm couldn't see it or a hiring manager couldn't be bothered to respond.

The silence is not a verdict on who you are.

It is a reflection of a system that was never truly built with you in mind.

Keep going. Not because resilience is a virtue the internet demands of you. But because you deserve to land somewhere that actually sees you.

The job market will not be fixed by a better algorithm. It will not be fixed by a shinier platform feature. It will not be fixed by another LinkedIn post about staying positive.

It will be fixed when the people inside the system remember — there are people in it.

One conversation at a time.

If this made you uncomfortable — good. Discomfort is where honesty begins. And honesty is where change follows.

Forward this to someone who needs to hear they are not broken. And to someone who needs to hear they have the power to do better.

SEO. GEO. AEO. And Whatever Comes Next.

Your brand is still broken, and technology isn't the reason. Every year, there is a new acronym. And through all of it, the foundation still matters most.

Every year, there is a new acronym.

First it was SEO. Get on Google. Optimise everything. Keywords, backlinks, meta descriptions. Rank on page one or you don't exist.

Then came GEO, Generative Engine Optimisation. Because now it's not just Google. It's AI answering questions before anyone clicks a link. ChatGPT. Perplexity. Gemini. Grok. Your brand either shows up in the AI's answer or it doesn't exist at all.

Then AEO, Answer Engine Optimisation. Because the search bar itself is evolving. People aren't typing queries anymore. They are asking questions and expecting answers. Instantly. Conversationally. Without visiting your carefully built website.

And now Search Everywhere Optimisation. Because search is no longer a destination. It happens on Instagram. On YouTube. On Reddit. On TikTok (minus India). On AI assistants people talk to while cooking dinner.

Every year. A new acronym. A new panic. A new agency selling you the next thing to fix.

And through all of it, your brand is still broken.

Not because you missed the trend.

Because you never fixed the foundation.

Technology does not build brands. It distributes them.

This is the line that would have saved a lot of expensive mistakes.

SEO can put you on page one. It cannot make someone care about what they find there.

GEO can get you cited by an AI. It cannot make that citation mean anything.

AEO can make you the answer to a question. It cannot make you the brand someone trusts enough to actually act on.

Search Everywhere Optimisation can put you on every surface simultaneously. But if your message is broken, you are just broken everywhere at once. Faster than ever. At scale.

The brands that have survived every revolution — and there have been many — did not survive because they were the fastest to adapt. They survived because they were absolutely clear on two things.

Their product. And their positioning.

Everything else was distribution.

Let me show you what that actually looks like.

Amul. Launched in 1946. Survived Partition, liberalisation, the internet, e-commerce, Instagram, and now AI. The Amul girl has been commenting on current affairs since 1966. Not because Amul has the best digital strategy in India with over 8000+ campaigns and still going strong. Because the product is trusted and the positioning, the taste of India, has never shifted. Every new medium just gave them a new canvas for the same story.

Fevicol. No technology has disrupted Fevicol. You know why? Because "unbreakable bond" is not a tagline. It is a promise that has been kept for over 60 years. SEO, GEO, AEO, Fevicol will show up on all of it. And their brand will mean exactly the same thing on every surface. Because they built something real long before they built anything digital.

Apple. In 1997, Apple was 90 days from bankruptcy. Steve Jobs came back and killed 70% of the product line. He did not launch a new technology strategy. He launched Think Different, a positioning statement. Two words. The technology followed the clarity. Not the other way around.

Nike. Nike does not sell shoes. It never did. It sells the belief that anyone can be an athlete. That single human truth has survived running booms, fitness apps, social media, influencer culture, and AI coaching tools. Because great positioning is not about the channel. It is about the truth underneath, the one that does not expire.

Tanishq. Launched in 1994 by Tata. Entered a market completely dominated by local jewellers with generational trust. Nobody believed a corporate brand could sell gold in India, jewellery here is emotional, familial, hyperlocal. Tanishq didn't out-product the competition. They out-positioned it. Through e-commerce, Instagram, and now lab-grown diamond conversations, the positioning hasn't moved. Trust, craftsmanship, Indian woman. Everything else was just a new channel for the same truth.

The pattern is the same every time. Clarity first. Distribution second.

So why is your brand still broken?

I say this with full respect because I have sat across enough boardrooms and founder conversations to know this is far more common than anyone publicly admits.

It is rarely the technology.

It is almost always one of these three things.

You don't have a clear point of view. You stand for everything, which means you stand for nothing. Your competitor could swap their logo onto your website and nobody would notice the difference.

You have confused activity with strategy. Posting, optimising, running ads, chasing every new algorithm and none of it connects to a single coherent story about why you exist and who you genuinely exist for.

You launched a GTM before you had a reason to exist. Go-To-Market strategy is not your brand strategy. GTM tells you how to reach the market. It does not tell the market why they should care. I have seen startups spend lakhs on performance marketing, influencer campaigns, and launch events, with a beautiful GTM deck and then wonder why nothing is sticking. Because GTM amplifies your positioning. It does not replace it. If the positioning is hollow, the GTM just burns money faster.

You are building distribution before you have built desire. You want to be everywhere before you have given people a real reason to care that you are anywhere.

This is the part that stings. I know. I have been here myself.

Here is what to do with all the acronyms.

Learn SEO. Adapt to GEO. Understand AEO. Show up wherever the next Search Everywhere demands.

But do all of it in service of a brand that already knows what it stands for.

Because technology is a megaphone.

And a megaphone pointed at a broken message does not fix the message.

It just makes it louder. On more platforms. In front of more people.

Faster than ever before.

Back to the question I opened with.

If every algorithm disappeared tomorrow, would people still find a way to get to you?

If the answer is yes, your brand is working. Protect it. The technology will keep changing. Your foundation should not.

If the answer is uncertain, that uncertainty is your brief. Not the next acronym. Not the next agency pitch. Not the next tool.

The real work is in the story you have not been honest enough to tell yet.

That is where every great brand started.

Including yours.

THE CMO'S BLIND SPOT!! The Problem Isn't the Creative. It Never Was.

The most expensive thing in marketing isn't a bad campaign. It's a great agency that doesn't know your product.

At JioSaavn, I once asked one of India's most reputed creative agencies to stop working. Completely. For a week.

No briefs. No decks. No social posts. Nothing.

We were stuck in a loop of weekly deliverables on their end, monthly billing on ours, and a growing pile of rejected creatives sitting between us like an awkward silence nobody wanted to name. The work was polished. Thoroughly, professionally polished. It just wasn't us.

Every concept came in confident. Every concept landed slightly off. Not wrong enough to put your finger on in a feedback call. Just, not us. Not the product that people loved at 7am on a run or 11pm when the city finally went quiet and they needed something that felt like home.

We would go back and forth. They would revise. We would push again. They would revise again. And somewhere in that loop, everyone was working very hard and nothing was actually moving.

I sat with it for a while before I could name what was wrong.

They knew us as a client. They didn't know us as a product.

There's a difference. A significant one. Knowing a brand means you have read the deck, attended the onboarding, absorbed the brand guidelines, noted the tone of voice document. Knowing a product means you have felt it. You have used it on a bad day.

That gap between knowing a brand and feeling it was showing up in every single creative we were rejecting.

So I called a pause.

One full week. No output. No deliverables. No pressure to produce.

Use the product if need be. Argue about it within their own team. Disagree. Get opinionated. Come back when they had instincts about it not just information.

My own team raised an eyebrow. We were already behind on the content calendar. Pausing felt like a luxury we couldn't afford.

It was the opposite of a luxury. It was the most efficient decision I made that quarter.

When the agency came back, something had genuinely shifted. The ideas had texture that wasn't there before. The language sounded like it came from inside the product, not outside it. The creative instincts were plugged into something real something felt, not just briefed. We lost a week of posts. We gained months of alignment.

I sent chocolates to the whole team in Mumbai.

Not as a gesture. As a genuine thank you for doing something that takes more courage than most people acknowledge, stopping, unlearning, and starting again from a place of real understanding.

We have built an entire industry infrastructure around the idea that the client-agency relationship is a production system. Weekly sprints. Monthly billing cycles. Quarterly reviews. Utilisation targets. The whole architecture is designed to optimise for output and it does that very efficiently.

And in that gap, we have created a culture where everyone is incentivised to keep producing. The agency needs to show work to justify the retainer. The client team needs to show approvals to justify their role. The calendar needs to be filled because an empty calendar looks like nothing is happening. So the creatives keep coming, the rejections keep piling, and everyone quietly points at the brief as the problem.

Longer brief. More detailed brief. More reference slides. More examples of what we don't want.

But I've sat across enough conference tables both from brand and agency side, to know that the brief is rarely where the problem actually lives. The problem lives in the distance between the agency and the product. And no document closes that distance.

So what does this mean practically?

Not every brand can afford to pause a week of output. But every brand can afford to ask a harder question before the next creative review:

Does this agency know our product the way someone who loves it would know it or do they know it the way someone who read about it would?

If it's the latter, no amount of brief refinement will fix what's actually broken.

The creative isn't the problem. The brief isn't the problem. The distance is.

The best agencies don't just get your brand. They get why it matters and that's something you have to give them the space to discover.

The Smartest Content Strategy Nobody Talks About!

You move further when you pull together. Don't just create content. Create it with someone.

Everyone is talking about content.

Post consistently. Build your brand. Show up every day. And most people nod, open a blank page, and freeze.

Because the hardest part of content was never the publishing. It was the what-do-I-even-say problem.

Here's the trick we have been using across every role I have held, long before personal branding became a LinkedIn religion.

Don't just create content. Create it with someone.

The numbers don't lie.

Collaborative content gets up to 3x more engagement than solo content.

Co-created content has built-in distribution, every contributor shares it with their own audience.

Some brands generate over 28% of their total revenue through partnerships, with long-term collaborations seeing annual growth exceeding 50%.

And yet, most personal brands and small marketing teams never touch it.

I have done this at every stage of my career.

At Tata Advanced Systems we partnered with DSCI and NASSCOM to co-publish industry content and get featured in their newsletters. We didn't build the audience from scratch. We showed up where the audience already was.

At JioSaavn we created a yearly marketing playbook with CMOs and marketing leaders across industries. Their names on the cover meant their networks became our reach. Their endorsement became our credibility.

Pepper Content came to me for a leadership series. Their platform, their audience, my thinking. I didn't build that stage, I just showed up prepared.

At Blue Vector, in-house podcast content built with creators. Not content about the brand. Content with people.

And there were many more. But the common thread, I never carried the full weight alone.

The brands that figured this out early.

McDonald's x Coca-Cola in 1955. Seventy years of co-marketing so seamless you can't imagine one without the other. That's what sustained collaborative content builds, default association in a consumer's mind.

GoPro x Red Bull. No new product. Just a content agreement. One got access to 1,800+ events across 100 countries. The other got footage no money could manufacture. Two audiences. One story.

Uber x Spotify. Two completely different products, one shared content moment. Over a billion combined users. Built entirely on showing up together.

So why isn't everyone doing this?

Because it requires something most content advice skips, relationships.

You have to reach out. Pitch something. Be willing to give before you get.

But here's what collaborative content does that solo content almost never can, it builds your network while you are creating, not after. It gives you credibility by association, honestly earned. It multiplies your reach without multiplying your effort.

One collaboration can open more doors than six months of posting alone.

How to start, like today:

You don't need a big brand or a big budget.

Look for someone building something complementary, not competitive.

Pitch a perspective, not a product, lead with what you can bring to the table.

Start small, a guest feature, a joint post, a podcast episode.

Make it genuinely useful for both audiences, not just a logo swap.

Let go of full control, co-creation means shared ownership, and that's the point.

The gateway is smaller than you think. The compounding is larger than you would expect.

The truth most people miss.

Collaborative content isn't a hack. It's how the best professional relationships get built by making something together.

It builds your network while you are creating, not after. It earns credibility by association, honestly. It multiplies your reach without multiplying your effort.

One collaboration can open more doors than six months of posting alone.

The blank page is always easier when someone is sitting across the table from you.

Who Moved My Cheese!

I have lived through three technological revolutions. One move, repeated. This isn't a survival story. It's a choice story.

This isn't a survival story. It's a choice story. And the choice was always the same, lean in before the wave crashes, not after.

Let me say something that doesn't get said out loud enough. Every technological shift doesn't just change how we work. It reshuffles who gets to stay in the room.

Readiness is a myth. You don't feel ready. You just go anyway. I have done it three times already. Here's what that actually looked like.

Evolution 01 - Computers.

My education didn't teach me computers. No syllabus covered it. No one in my circle was rushing toward it. But something in me understood that the world was quietly, irreversibly changing and standing still wasn't a neutral act.

So I enrolled into courses myself, even if they weren't available in my city. DTC bus to Connaught Place. Simple as that. I didn't wait for it to come to me.

Relevance is a decision you make before the market forces it on you.

Evolution 02 - The Internet.

Being online wasn't cool yet. We did it anyway.

We were early internet adopters at home, that irritating dial-up sound we all remember, at a time when most people still considered it a novelty. And when social media arrived, the same instinct kicked in.

The internet didn't just give you information. It gave you reach. The ability to be known before you walked into a room. To build a reputation that arrived ahead of you.

The people who sat that one out believed their work would speak for itself. In a world with infinite noise, invisible equals irrelevant.

A decade into my career, I took on the social media lead role for Hult Prize in 2012 when social media was still very fresh. Not for the title. Because I wanted to understand how digital communities actually work, from the inside.

You don't learn that by watching. You learn it by doing even when you are overqualified for the doing.

Staying relevant sometimes means volunteering to be the least experienced person in the room. Again. Ego has no seat at the table of evolution.

Evolution 03 - AI.

Same crossroads. Same choice. Different decade. Higher stakes.

I will be direct, I am already using AI where it makes sense for my work. And I am actively telling the people around me: use it. If it saves you three hours of research, use it. If it helps you draft faster so you can think deeper, use it.

The shift is real. It is not a phase. And the anxiety most people are feeling is mostly misdirected.

There's a growing chorus of voices, podcasters, influencers, self-proclaimed futurists, actively telling people that being a carpenter, gardener, or plumber is the safe career move now. Let's be honest about what's actually being said: don't compete with AI, retreat to the physical. That's not a strategy. That's managed surrender. And the people offering that advice? They are not retreating anywhere.

The question was never "which jobs will AI take?"

The right question is: who is going to get the seat on this musical chair that never stops playing?

It won't be the person who used AI to think less. It will be the person who used AI to think better and showed up with judgment, context, and the irreplaceable human layer on top.

That's not optimism. That's pattern recognition. I have seen this movie twice already.

The through-line.

The cheese will keep moving. That's just the world now.

Every revolution asks the same question: are you willing to be a beginner again?

Most aren't. Not because they lack intelligence. Because being good at something for years creates an invisible gravity. It feels safer to defend the depth you have than to start over shallow.

Three shifts. One move, repeated. Each time I leaned in, I briefly became the least experienced person in the room again. Uncomfortable? Yes. The price of staying? Also yes.

You can move with the cheese or sit where it used to be and go hungry. That's the whole game.

AI is here. It isn't leaving. Adapt or adopt but do it sooner rather than later. The ones who wait for certainty before they move are the ones who arrive after the cheese is already gone.

The music never stops.

Find the bus. Get on it. Figure out the rest when you arrive.

The Academia-Industry Gap Is Not a Problem. It's a Choice.

Why does a 22-year-old with a management degree sometimes struggle to write a professional email, but can quote Porter's Five Forces in their sleep?

And both sides are choosing, every single day, to keep it wide open.

Here's an uncomfortable question:

Why does a 22-year-old with a management degree sometimes struggle to write a professional email, but can quote Porter's Five Forces in their sleep?

This isn't a talent problem. It's a design problem.

And the design has been broken for a while.

The gap is real. Let's name it.

The academia-industry gap isn't a rumour. It's a lived experience on both sides.

Ask a hiring manager, and they will tell you: "Fresh graduates come in theory-heavy and context-light."

Ask a professor, and they will say: "We are building thinkers, not just workers."

Both are right. Both are also talking past each other.

Why does it exist?

Not because academia is lazy. Not because industry is impatient.

Because both systems were built for entirely different gods and have never been forced to reconcile. Academia rewards depth, research, and theory. Industry rewards speed, judgment, and output. Neither is wrong. Both are incomplete.

And in between sits a student paying significant money, investing years of their life, trusting that the bridge exists.

It largely doesn't.

Curricula revise every 2-3 years. Markets move every quarter. By the time a case study enters a classroom, the company it's about has already pivoted twice.

Meanwhile, industry shows up on campus twice a year once for a guest lecture, once for placements. That's not partnership. That's extraction.

What it's actually costing.

Students pay first. They graduate carrying credentials that signal potential but don't demonstrate capability. The rude awakening hits in the first job when someone asks them to just do it and the classroom map doesn't match the terrain.

That paralysis isn't a character flaw. It's a design flaw.

Companies pay second. Re-training is never budgeted for but always happening. I have seen founders quietly stop hiring freshers not because they don't believe in young talent, but because the ratio of investment to readiness has made it feel risky.

That's a structural failure, not a talent failure.

Institutions pay last, but they will pay. In a world where a motivated 20-year-old can learn from the best minds on the planet for free, the value of a degree must be in experience, exposure, and ecosystem. Not just the certificate.

Relevance is not permanent.

What actually fixes it.

Here's what I have seen move the needle from working directly with students across academic bodies and alongside new-age brands building in real time.

Live briefs over case studies. Students handed a real brand problem, real deadline, real stakeholder, think differently. I have run sessions where students diagnosed an actual problem for a brand. Some of the recommendations were even implemented. That's not studying business. That's doing it.

Practitioners as co-designers, not guest speakers. There's a difference between someone who comes in for 90 minutes and someone who shapes what questions students are even taught to ask. The institutions doing this well have advisory councils that meet quarterly and have real influence over curriculum.

Immersion over internships. A two-month internship making decks is not immersion. Being present when a brand makes a hard call, a product kill, a pivot, a difficult customer situation that's where judgment is built. Proximity to real decisions is not teachable. It has to be lived.

The action plan.

Academic institutions:

Introduce live brand briefs as a graded component every semester.

Build practitioner advisory boards with actual curriculum influence, not ceremonial titles.

Measure graduate employability as seriously as research rankings.

Industry:

Stop showing up only at placement season, engage earlier, consistently, with something to offer.

Open real problems to students: capstones, advisory projects, live feedback loops.

Tell institutions honestly what's missing. They are often willing to change, they just don't have the signal.

Students:

Don't wait for the system to be fixed before you start building.

Seek proximity to real decisions, aggressively.

Your degree gives you the framework. You build the fluency yourself.

The bottom line.

Industry has taken a step forward, imperfectly, but forward.

Some institutions are taking theirs.

When both sides finally meet, not at a panel, not in a press release, but in actual curriculum and actual commitment, the talent pipeline won't just improve.

It will transform.

But until then, every student sitting in a classroom that doesn't know what the market needs right now is paying the price of a conversation two systems haven't had yet.

That's not okay.

Let's change it.
